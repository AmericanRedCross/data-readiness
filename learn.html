An overview of data readiness

In the Red Cross Red Crescent Movement context, data readiness is the ability of National Societies to use quality and timely information to influence operations and planning to respond effectively to disasters.

Below describes the three main components of data readiness.

Data Literacy

Data Literacy includes the skills, knowledge, attitudes, and social structures required for different populations to use data.

Data Preparedness

Data Preparedness includes the ability to responsibly and effectively deploy and manage data collection and analysis tools, techniques and strategies in a specific operational context before a disaster strikes.

Operational IM

Operational IM includes the ability to use data for response operation decisions and have reliably integrated data into both design and implementation.

Sample competencies

To meet the main objectives under data readiness, there are a number of competencies a team needs to have. These range everywhere from building a data culture, to having the right data collection tools, to making data-driven decisions.

Explore some of the different competencies below.

Data literacy skills

Skills and capacity to collect, clean, store, and interpret.

Data collection systems

Ability to collect information from individuals and groups, including surveys, procedures, and equipment.

Data sharing

Having a system for active exchange of information, including: internally, between organizations, and in the public domain.

Secondary data

The capacity to collect additional information needed for an operation (e.g. rapid assessments) to complement existing secondary data.

Theory of change

All the components and priority competencies are part of a broader Theory of Change (ToC) on data readiness for operations and planning. If you're interested, go ahead and review the ToC. You can use it as a reference for learning, strategy development, proposal writing, or anything else.
